# Honolulu Marathon Training Plan

**Race:** JAL Honolulu Marathon — Sunday, December 13, 2026, 5:00 AM start
**Runner:** Brandon (44) — run days Mon / Wed / Sat
**Goal:** Finish comfortably (no time pressure — there is no cutoff)
**Starting point:** ~5–6 mile longest run, ~26 weeks out

---

## The five rules that make this work

1. **Easy means easy.** Almost every mile here is at conversational effort — heart rate roughly **140–150**, capped around 150. If you can't speak full sentences, slow down or walk. This is the opposite of how you've been running, and it's what lets the long runs keep growing without wrecking you.

2. **Run/walk from the start.** Practice a ratio in training (e.g. run 4 min / walk 1 min) so it's automatic on race day. Walk breaks lower your heart rate and core temp — in Honolulu's heat they often make you *faster* overall, not slower.

3. **Cutback weeks are training, not slacking.** Every 4th week the long run drops back so your body absorbs the work. Don't skip them — they're where you actually get stronger and where injuries get avoided.

4. **The long run is the priority.** Monday and Wednesday are just easy support miles. If you're tired, shorten Mon/Wed before you touch Saturday.

5. **The foot gates the ramp.** This plan assumes your MRI and podiatrist clear you to build. The early weeks are deliberately conservative to fit around that. If the foot flares, back off and check with them — and know that capping your longest run at **18** instead of 20 is totally fine for finishing.

## Pacing & heart-rate reference (max HR ~185)

- **Easy / all training runs:** ~140–150 bpm — conversational, slightly-too-slow-feeling
- **Race-day target:** ~148–158 bpm, managed with walk breaks
- **Don't chase pace.** Same effort gets faster on its own over the months as your aerobic base builds.

---

## Optional marathon-effort segments (★ weeks)

Three Wednesdays (marked ★ in the table) swap part of your easy run for a stretch at **marathon effort** — HR ~148–158, "comfortably hard but controlled." You could get out a short sentence, but you wouldn't want to chat. It sits one notch above easy. These are optional, but they teach your legs and head what race-day effort actually feels like, so it isn't a surprise in December.

Same 6-mile Wednesday distance — you're only changing the effort of the middle:

- **Week 13 (Sep 12) — first taste:** 2 mi easy / **2 mi @ marathon effort** / 2 mi easy
- **Week 18 (Oct 17) — build it:** 1.5 mi easy / **3 mi @ marathon effort** / 1.5 mi easy
- **Week 21 (Nov 7) — final tune-up:** 1 mi easy / **4 mi @ marathon effort** / 1 mi easy

Run the effort stretch using your race-day run/walk ratio — that makes it the most race-specific rehearsal you can get. If you only do one or two, do the later ones. And skip them if your foot or fatigue says so; they're a confidence tool, not a requirement.

## The other days

- **Tue / Thu:** your weights / rowing / yoga — keep them genuinely *easy* so they don't eat into run recovery.
- **Friday:** rest or light yoga before Saturday's long run. Going into the long run fresh matters more than an extra workout.
- Skip or shorten cross-training the day before any long run from October on.

---

## Heat prep (the Honolulu-specific piece)

You'll train through a WA summer and fall — cooler and drier than race morning. You can't fully replicate Honolulu, but in the final 6–8 weeks consider a few runs slightly overdressed or on a treadmill in a warm room to teach your body to cool itself. Mostly, just go in *mentally* prepared for heat to slow you, and lean hard on the walk breaks, fluids, and ice on race day.

---

## Race morning quick notes

- 5:00 AM dark start — go out **easy and restrained**; the first 6 miles should feel boring.
- Run/walk from mile 1, don't wait until you're tired.
- HR ~148–158, run by effort as it heats up.
- Cool aggressively: fluids, ice, sponges at every aid station.
- No clock pressure. Everyone finishes. Your only job is to keep moving.
