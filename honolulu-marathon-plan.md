# Honolulu Marathon Training Plan

**Race:** JAL Honolulu Marathon — Sunday, December 13, 2026, 5:00 AM start
**Runner:** Brandon (44) — run days Mon / Wed / Sat
**Goal:** Finish comfortably (no time pressure — there is no cutoff)
**Starting point:** ~5–6 mile longest run, ~26 weeks out

---

## ⚠️ Knee revision — July 2026

Brandon's knee flared, and the physical therapist called for **a one-month pause from running (Jul 11 – Aug 1) to build leg strength**, with rowing or biking for cardio. This version of the plan reflects that:

- **Weeks 4–7 are a rowing block** — no running. Each row **matches the duration and easy effort (HR 140–150) of the run it replaces**, so the aerobic engine is maintained. PT leg-strength work is **daily**.
- **Weeks 8–10 are a walk/run return-to-run ramp**, rebuilding impact tolerance gradually while easy rows hold the aerobic base.
- **The long run now peaks at 18 miles** (Nov 21) instead of 20 — lower joint stress, and plenty for a no-clock finish.
- **The knee gates the ramp.** If pain returns, back off and check with the PT before pushing on.

Good news: rowing preserves almost all of your aerobic fitness. The only thing a month off running costs is *impact conditioning* — bone, tendon, and running economy — which is exactly what the walk/run ramp rebuilds.

---

## The five rules that make this work

1. **Easy means easy.** Almost every mile — and every rowing minute — is at conversational effort — heart rate roughly **140–150**, capped around 150. If you can't speak full sentences, slow down or walk (or ease the stroke rate). This is the opposite of how you've been running, and it's what lets the long runs keep growing without wrecking you.

2. **Run/walk from the start.** Practice a ratio in training (e.g. run 4 min / walk 1 min) so it's automatic on race day. Walk breaks lower your heart rate and core temp — in Honolulu's heat they often make you *faster* overall, not slower. The return-to-run ramp is built entirely on run/walk.

3. **Cutback weeks are training, not slacking.** Every 4th week the long run drops back so your body absorbs the work. Don't skip them — they're where you actually get stronger and where injuries get avoided.

4. **The long run is the priority.** Monday and Wednesday are just easy support miles. If you're tired, shorten Mon/Wed before you touch Saturday.

5. **The knee (and foot) gate the ramp.** This plan assumes the PT and podiatrist clear you to build. The rowing block and walk/run ramp are deliberately conservative for exactly this reason. If the knee or foot talks back, back off and check with them — capping your longest run at **18** is already baked in, and going even shorter is totally fine for finishing.

## The rowing block (Weeks 4–7) — same effort, same time, no impact

The idea, in your words: **same duration and heart-rate target, rowing instead of running.** Each session mirrors the run it would have been:

- **Effort:** steady HR **140–150**, conversational. This is *easy* rowing — resist the urge to hammer the erg.
- **Duration:** matches the equivalent run's time on feet. Mon/Wed are short (35–50 min); Saturday is your "long row," building **50 → 70 min** over the four weeks. The long row is capped near 70 min on purpose — you don't need to match a 90-minute run on the erg to hold aerobic fitness, and the seat/back thank you for it.
- **No ratio to hit — just steady easy effort.** Don't replicate the 4:1 run/walk on the rower. Your walk breaks are a *running* tool (they manage impact and heat and rehearse race day); the erg has no impact and isn't race-specific, so simply row steadily in the 140–150 zone for the whole session. If a long row gets monotonous, an occasional easy paddle is fine — but it's optional. The only target is time in the HR zone. You'll get real run/walk practice back in Weeks 8–10.
- **PT leg-strength: every day.** This is the whole point of the pause — the strength work is the priority, the rows keep the engine warm.
- **Bike swap:** if the knee prefers cycling on a given day, swap a row for an equal-duration easy spin. Rowing stays the default.

By the end of Week 7 you'll have held nearly all of your aerobic fitness with zero running impact — the ideal setup to start running again.

## Return to run (Weeks 8–10) — rebuild the impact tolerance

The engine is fine; the tissues need to relearn running. So we ramp with walk/run:

- **Week 8:** run **1 min / walk 2 min**, short. ~2 mi feel on Mon/Wed, ~3 mi Saturday. Keep 1–2 easy 30–40 min rows on off-days to hold aerobic volume.
- **Week 9:** run **2 min / walk 1 min**, a little longer. Still rowing on off-days.
- **Week 10:** back to your usual **run 4′ / walk 1′** ratio (the same one you'll use every run and on race day), with a normal Saturday long run (~7 mi). Rowing tapers off to one session as running volume returns.

**The knee gates this.** Any sharp or lingering knee pain → stop, ice, and check with the PT before continuing the ramp. There is no rush; the race has no clock.

## Pacing & heart-rate reference (max HR ~185)

- **Easy / all training — runs and rows:** ~140–150 bpm — conversational, slightly-too-slow-feeling
- **Race-day target:** ~148–158 bpm, managed with walk breaks
- **Don't chase pace.** Same effort gets faster on its own over the months as your aerobic base builds.

## Optional marathon-effort segments (★ weeks)

Three Wednesdays (marked ★) swap part of your easy run for a stretch at **marathon effort** — HR ~148–158, "comfortably hard but controlled." You could get out a short sentence, but you wouldn't want to chat. It sits one notch above easy. These are optional, but they teach your legs and head what race-day effort actually feels like. They now sit later in the plan, once running is fully re-established:

- **Week 17 (Oct 10) — first taste:** 2 mi easy / **2 mi @ marathon effort** / 2 mi easy
- **Week 20 (Oct 31) — build it:** 1.5 mi easy / **3 mi @ marathon effort** / 1.5 mi easy
- **Week 22 (Nov 14) — final tune-up:** 1 mi easy / **4 mi @ marathon effort** / 1 mi easy

Run the effort stretch using your race-day run/walk ratio — that makes it the most race-specific rehearsal you can get. If you only do one or two, do the later ones. And skip them if the knee, foot, or fatigue says so; they're a confidence tool, not a requirement.

## The other days

- **PT leg-strength: daily** through the rowing block, then keep it going 2–3×/week once you're running again — it's what protects the knee for the rest of the build.
- **Tue / Thu:** during the rowing block these are strength + optional easy rows/bike; once running, keep any cross-training genuinely *easy* so it doesn't eat into run recovery.
- **Friday:** rest or light yoga before Saturday's long session. Going in fresh matters more than an extra workout.
- Skip or shorten cross-training the day before any long run from October on.

## Heat prep (the Honolulu-specific piece)

You'll train through a WA summer and fall — cooler and drier than race morning. You can't fully replicate Honolulu, but in the final 6–8 weeks consider a few runs slightly overdressed or on a treadmill in a warm room to teach your body to cool itself. Mostly, just go in *mentally* prepared for heat to slow you, and lean hard on the walk breaks, fluids, and ice on race day.

## Race morning quick notes

- 5:00 AM dark start — go out **easy and restrained**; the first 6 miles should feel boring.
- Run/walk from mile 1, don't wait until you're tired.
- HR ~148–158, run by effort as it heats up.
- Cool aggressively: fluids, ice, sponges at every aid station.
- No clock pressure. Everyone finishes. Your only job is to keep moving.
